# EIS Master Rulebook

Consolidated rules and concepts for the **Equal Interval System** (Lyle "Spud" Murphy) / Murphy System, merged from:
- Ted Greene's handwritten-note PDFs (1977–1978) in `/EIS/`
- Public web material about Murphy's system (collected 2026-04-18; see sources at bottom)

Provenance tags inline: `[TG]` = directly from Ted Greene's PDF pages, `[Web]` = from public descriptions of Murphy's system, `[Both]` = stated in both. Page references `p.XX` are from Ted Greene's pages (i.e. Spud Murphy's original book pagination as copied by Greene).

---

## 0. What EIS is

- Also called: *System of Horizontal Composition Based on Equal Intervals*. `[Both]`
- Developed by Lyle "Spud" Murphy (1908–2005), arranger for Benny Goodman and later a film/TV composer in Los Angeles. `[Web]`
- 12-volume course, originally ~1,200 pages, now ~1,415. `[Web]`
- Derived from the **overtone series** ("nature's overtones"); Murphy got the original idea from the trumpet's 6 valve combinations and its overblowing of the overtones. `[Web]`
- Explicitly **not** related to Schoenberg, Slonimsky, or Schillinger. `[Web]`
- Style-agnostic: pop, jazz, classical, film, serial, polytonal all live in the same framework. `[Web]`
- Murphy's framing: the whole method uses "about half a dozen rules" plus practice. `[Web]`

## 1. The Three Basic Principles (to master) `[Both, p.123]`

1. Equal-interval Root tones (the E-cycles; see §4).
2. The 18 scales.
3. The vertical chord structures built from those scales.

Plus the practical goal: **weave melodic and harmonic lines through them**.

## 2. The Method (procedure) `[TG, p.129]`

1. Begin with **Root tones** (pick an E-cycle or a mixture).
2. **Choose the scales** over each Root (one of the 18).
3. **Build the harmonies** vertically from the chosen scale.
4. Get the final result via **voice-leading, non-chord tones, resolutions, and reverse resolutions**.

A melody may be:
- a single line with harmony in the bass clef (e.g. `15 / 10`),
- or 2P-H3, 3P, 4P, 5P, or complete chords.

## 3. Global principles `[TG / Both]`

- **Everything in this course is reversible.** Any passage can be played backwards and still make musical sense. `[TG p.34]`
- **"The higher you go, the freer you get."** `[TG p.49]`
- **"Mark everything — it will come in handy for reference later."** `[TG p.24]`
- There is **no key** as such; all Root tones are of equal importance. A tonal center can exist if you want one. `[Both, p.3 numbered]`
- **Bar line as an integrating force** `[TG p.19 numbered]`: everything inside a single bar shares one Root tone, scale, chord, and NCT set. VL and root-progression concerns only apply **across** the bar line.

## 4. Root lines — the Equal-Interval ("E-number") cycles `[Both]`

Murphy reduces the 12 chromatic intervals to **6 equal-interval cycles** (an interval and its inversion share one cycle because the Root tones are octave-equivalent).

| E# | Interval | Cycle length | Example cycle (ascending from C) |
|----|----------|--------------|------------------------------------|
| E1 | minor 2nd (semitone) | 12 | C → C# → D → … (chromatic) |
| E2 | major 2nd (whole tone) | 6 | C → D → E → F# → G# → A# |
| E3 | minor 3rd | 4 | C → E♭ → G♭ → A |
| E4 | major 3rd | 3 | C → E → G# |
| E5 | perfect 4th | 12 | C → F → B♭ → E♭ → … (circle of 4ths) |
| E6 | tritone | 2 | C → F# |

- The Perfect Fifth is **treated as an octave position of a 4th** — so there is no separate "E7"; fifths live inside E5 (descending or inverted). `[TG item 7 numbered]`
- When several Root tones appear in sequence they are called a **Progression**. The term *progression* refers only to the horizontal movement of the Root tones (in the bass). `[TG p.26 / item 6]`
- **Elision** `[TG item 21 numbered]`: you may leave out any intervals in the bass not necessary to your pattern or not conforming to the metric structure in the treble.
- In E5, E4, E3, and E6, the bass notes **may be in any order of appearance** between Root tones. `[TG item 20 numbered]`

## 5. Scales (18 scales) `[Both]`

- Murphy uses **18 scales**, each based on the Natural Major scale but with diatonic intervals expanded. `[TG item 8 numbered]`
- The scales are numbered (e.g. Scale #1, #3, #7, #8, #9, #10, #11, etc. are referenced by Ted Greene); #10 is explicitly the scale generating Dominant 7♭9 shapes. `[TG Writing pg.]`
- Good **4th-chord scales**: 1, 3, 4, 7 (11, 12 in 4-part); possibly 4+. `[TG p.152A]`

(Greene's notes don't list all 18 by name — that's in the actual Murphy books.)

## 6. Overtone-series foundation of vertical spacing `[TG item 11–13 numbered]`

Vertical spacing follows the overtone series as closely as possible. Dividing the overtone series into 5 octaves:

| Octave | Content | Label |
|--------|---------|-------|
| 1 | fundamental + its octave | — |
| 2 | Root and 5th | — |
| 3 | 1, 3, 5, ♭7 | Chord Tones |
| 4 | 1, 2, 3, 4+, 5, 6, ♭7 | Scale Tones |
| 5 | 1, 2, 3, 4+, 5, 6, ♭7 | Scale Tones |

- **Harmony** = a set of tones from one scale sounded simultaneously. All chords are built on certain intervals belonging to the various scales. `[TG item 9]`
- Use a **Two-Octave Scale** when building chords. `[TG item 10]`

## 7. Voice-Leading (VL) `[TG p.17, Both]`

- Hold **common tones**.
- **Contrary motion** between one or more parts and the bass.
- Move **remaining tones** to the nearest tones in the new chord.
- **No three tones together** (no matter how voiced in several octaves). `[TG p.88, 97]`
- **Non-chord tones do not affect the voice-leading**; they sit between two chord tones. `[TG p.26, item 17]`
- In chords containing ♮7, the ♮7 **must always resolve (to 6)** whenever 9 resolves to 8. `[TG p.127]`

Related terms:
- **C.O.P.** — Change of Position (treble inversion, applied across the bar line). Use voice-leading when crossing the bar. `[TG item 22]`
- **S.P.** — Substitute Position (used when **no tone is held over** in the same voice from one chord to the next; needed sometimes to keep a line going). `[TG item 23]`
- **S.V.L.** — Substitute Voice-Leading.
- Dominant-7th / minor-7th VL specifically recognizes: normal VL; brackets `[ ]` (where one tone will always be missing); the moves `3↔♭7`, `1→1`; `5→5`; same-bar `R to 5` with brackets on 3, ♭7; parallel VL; S.V.L / S.P.

## 8. Range / register `[TG p.15]`

- **No harmony tone written below the 3rd space of the bass clef** (except the lowest Root or 5th).
- The range limit may be **extended when the bass is in motion** (e.g. a bass solo). `[TG item 18]`

## 9. Outside-Octave Dissonance (OOD) `[TG p.97]`

- Generally **avoid OOD**, with these exceptions:
  - ♭9 is OK **if ♭7 is in the chord**.
  - ♭2 is OK **over a pedal**.
  - 3 & 11 are **not good**; 4 & 10 are fine.
  - Same pattern for 11 & 17.

## 10. Non-Chord Tones (NCTs) — six types `[TG Resources/Devices p.1]`

| Abbrev. | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| P.T. | Passing Tone |
| C.A. | Chromatic Alteration |
| R.T. | Returning Tone (neighbor) |
| C.T. | Chord Tone (used as a passing/substitute chord tone, e.g. in bass) |
| Sus. | Suspension |
| Ant. | Anticipation |

- NCTs **substitute for or alter** the chord tones (pp.23, 42–44).
- **Substitute Tones (ST's)** can replace chord tones — e.g. `6 for 5`, `4 for 3`, `2 for 1`, `9 for 3`. `[TG item 26]`
  - When ST's appear first and resolve back to the chord tone → **Leading Tones**.
  - When the chord tones appear first and ST's follow → **Passing Tones**.
- NCTs may be used in many combinations **or singly**.

## 11. Bass in Motion `[TG Resources/Devices #6, item 18, Both]`

1. Ascending or descending.
2. Modes: regular / free / elision / passing 3rds / passing 5ths / leading tones.
3. **Ascending Passing Thirds**: the Root tone must appear 1st at all times.
4. **Passing Fifths**: no ordering constraint.
5. **Passing 3rds & 5ths combined**: if the 3rd is ever sounded metrically at the same time as the treble structure, **double the Root or 5th in the treble and omit the 3rd**.
6. **Passing 3rds & 5ths + Equal Intervals** may be combined (pp.32–33).
7. Chord-Tone bass and the Equal-Interval bass may both combine with **Leading Tones** and **Passing Tones** (`2→1, 4→3, 1→2, 3→4`).
8. Any bass tone may be **repeated an octave higher or lower** (e.g. `T 8-3 5`).
9. **Watch for parallel octaves** between the bass and any note in the treble.
10. The movement of the bass through an ascending progression is exactly the opposite of the descending form — reversibility applies.
11. **Descending**: use diatonic motion on p.5 (E7), EIS on all other.
12. **Ascending**: likewise, but the last tone in the bar is the Root tone (except for E2^).

## 12. Bass & Treble Motion Combined `[TG item 25]`

The bass may move **and** the treble change position at the same time.

## 13. The Six-Four chord `[TG item 28]`

- Treated as a **separate chord with the Root in the bass** in this system.
- Standard resolution: `6→5, 4→3` (or the reverse); in minor, either 5 or 6.
- 6 and 4 are treated as Substitute Tones here.

## 14. Resolutions / Reverse Resolutions `[TG Writing pg.]`

- Every chord class (Natural 7th, Dominant 7, minor 7, 9th, 11th, 13th, etc.) has its own **resolutions and reverse resolutions**.
- **Mixing scales** across the resolution is allowed and encouraged (e.g. Scale #8 → Scale #9, p.128).
- Resolution is a primary structural tool alongside voice-leading and NCTs.

## 15. Writing resources (Greene's summary list) `[TG Writing pg.1]`

All of the following are first-class composing resources:

1. **Triad Progressions** (major & minor) in all ascending/descending Symmetric Cycles = all the E-progressions.
   - a) close, b) open, c) switching from one to the other.
   - 3 inversions / positions each.
   - VL, S.V.L., S.P. (and 1c above).
2. **Non-Chord Tones** in many combinations or singly.
3. **Substitute Tones**.
4. **Six-Four chords** — their resolutions and reverse resolutions.
5. **Treble C.O.P.** (with or without S.P.) across the bar line.
6. **Bass in Motion** (ascending/descending; regular/free/elision/passing 3rds/5ths/leading tones).
7. **Bass and Treble Motion combined.**
8. **C.O.P. of any one chord connection or progression** (ascending, descending, mixed).
   - E.g. `C/E – F/F, C/G – F/A`, or `C–G–Am, C–G–Am, C–G–Am`.

Many of these can be **combined**.

## 16. Chord classes, parts (2P/3P/4P), and voicing stocks `[TG Writing pg.2–3]`

General pattern for every chord class: treat it in close and open voicings, across all inversions, with VL, S.V.L., S.P., resolutions/reverse resolutions, C.O.P., NCTs, bass in motion.

Classes explicitly listed:

- **Triads** — major & minor, open & close, 3 inversions.
- **Natural 7ths** in major & minor — plus substitute tones (9 for 3).
- **4P Natural 9ths (ᐞ9), m♮9, 6th, m6th** — VL moves ↑, ↓, `[ ]` or parallel.
- **Dominant 7ths** (2P / 3P / 4P). VL also called "Changing Position", "Triad VL", "3-Parts VL", "Opposite VL":
  1. Normal VL
  2. Brackets `[ ]` (one tone will always be missing)
  3. `3 → ♭7`, `♭7 → 3`, `1 → 1`
  4. `3 → ♭7`, `♭7 → 3`, `5 → 5`
  5. Same-bar `R → 5` with `[ ]` on 3, ♭7
  6. Parallel
  7. S.V.L. or S.P.
- **Minor 7ths** (and `m7♭5`, `ᐞ7+`, `7♭5`, etc.; 2P `ᐞ7, mᐞ7, 6, m6`).
- **Minor 9ths** (3P & 4P).
- **Dominant 7♭9** (Scale #10).
- **Dominant 9ths** (2P / 3P / 4P). Different 3P → different tones may be added to bass (in parallel). **6 may be substituted for 7** in all 9th, 11th, 13th chords.
- **Dominant 13ths**, Types 1 & 2 (and 4+), in 3P and 4P. **"Only ♭10 with 11, or 10 with 11+"**. "Or 11 for 10." Scales: #1, #1 w/ 4+, #3, #3 w/ 4+, #7, #7 w/ 4+, #8, #9, #10, #10 w/ 4+, #11 Type 2.
- **Dominant 11th and Dominant 11th 4+** (3P & 4P). Or 11th from Scale #9 or #10. Parallel bass options. For 11+, also 3 in parallel with bass.
- **5-part chords** (mentioned but cut off in scan).

## 17. Advanced material covered in the 12-volume course `[Web]`

Beyond the elementary rules above, the Murphy course progresses into:

- **Quartal (4th-based), quintal (5th-based), and secondal (2nd-based) harmony.**
- **Polytonality.**
- **Interval relations** across parts.
- **Music with no chords and no scales.**
- **"Tropes"** — Murphy's version of serialism.
- **Orchestration and arrangement techniques** applying all of the above to real ensembles.

At this level, the elementary rules (range, OOD, etc.) progressively relax — the beginning rules exist to teach smooth voice-leading and tonal writing; later the student is taught to be much freer. `[Per Dan Sawyer's annotation]`
