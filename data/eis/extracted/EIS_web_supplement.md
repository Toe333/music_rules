# EIS — Web Supplement

Supplementary notes from public web sources (about the Equal Interval System, not from the Ted Greene PDFs). Collected 2026-04-18.

These are synthesized paraphrases — not direct quotes — of publicly available descriptions of Spud Murphy's system. For anything to be used *authoritatively*, fall back to the Ted Greene source PDFs or the actual 12-volume course.

---

## Origin of the system

- Developed by Lyle "Spud" Murphy (Miko Stephanovic, 1908–2005), longtime arranger (Benny Goodman, film/TV) and educator.
- Full name of the method: *System of Horizontal Composition Based on Equal Intervals*.
- Murphy derived the approach from the overtone series ("nature's overtones"), and got the initial spark from the trumpet's 6 valve combinations and overblowing of the overtone series.
- Explicitly not related to Schoenberg, Slonimsky, or Schillinger twelve-tone systems.
- Course began with "Book I. Basic Theory" and "Book II. System of Progressions"; grew over Murphy's lifetime to 12 volumes (originally ~1,200 pages, now ~1,415).

## Core claim

- The whole method is taught with, in Murphy's own framing, only "about half a dozen rules" plus practice. Everything else flows from applying those rules to different materials.
- The system is style-agnostic: it can handle pop, jazz, classical, film scoring, serial, polytonal — all within the same framework.

## What the 12 books cover (public descriptions)

- Open and close triads (major and minor).
- Natural 7th chords.
- 9th, 11th, 13th chords in various parts (2P / 3P / 4P — two-, three-, four-part voicings).
- Quartal (4th-based), quintal (5th-based), and secondal (2nd-based) harmony.
- Polytonality.
- Interval relations across parts.
- Music with no chords and no scales.
- "Tropes" — Murphy's version of serialism.
- Everything from the simplest pop song up to complex serial/polytonal orchestral writing.

## The Equal Interval ("E-number") shorthand

Murphy reduces the 12 chromatic intervals to **6 equal-interval cycles** (an interval and its inversion share one cycle, since the bass Root tones just cycle the octave).

Based on the Ted Greene material + web descriptions, the mapping is:

| E# | Interval | Cycle length | Example cycle (ascending from C) |
|----|----------|--------------|------------------------------------|
| E1 | minor 2nd (semitone) | 12 | C → C# → D → … (chromatic) |
| E2 | major 2nd (whole tone) | 6 | C → D → E → F# → G# → A# |
| E3 | minor 3rd | 4 | C → E♭ → G♭ → A |
| E4 | major 3rd | 3 | C → E → G# |
| E5 | perfect 4th | 12 | C → F → B♭ → E♭ → … (circle of 4ths) |
| E6 | tritone | 2 | C → F# |

Confirmed by Dan Sawyer's note: "E5 descending" = chord progression descending in perfect 4ths; "E3" = moving in minor 3rds. Murphy treats the perfect 5th as an "octave position of a fourth" — so there's no separate "E7"; the fifth is already contained in E5 descending (or E5 inverted).

Every Root tone in an E-progression is a **tonic** — there is no single key; all Roots are of equal harmonic weight. A piece can still have a "tonal center" if the composer wants one.

## Three Basic Principles (core of the system)

Directly echoed in Ted Greene's PDFs (p.123) but also restated in web material:

1. Equal-interval Root tones (the six E-cycles above).
2. The 18 scales (built on each Root).
3. The vertical chord structures built from those scales.

Plus: the practical goal — weaving melodic and harmonic lines through them.

## The Method (expressed as a procedure)

1. Choose a Root line (an E-cycle, or a mix/elision of them).
2. Choose the scale over each Root (one of the 18).
3. Build harmony vertically from that scale.
4. Get to the final result via voice-leading, non-chord tones, resolutions, and reverse resolutions.

## Voice-leading (VL) — restated

- Hold common tones.
- Contrary motion between one or more parts and the bass.
- Move remaining tones to the nearest tones in the new chord.
- Non-chord tones do not affect VL (they sit between two chord tones).
- "Everything in the course is reversible" — any passage can be read backwards and still make sense.

## Range / register constraint (from Greene p.15)

- No harmony tone written below the 3rd space of the bass clef (except the lowest Root or 5th).
- Range limit can be extended when the bass is in motion (e.g. a bass solo).

## Outside-Octave Dissonance (OOD) — compact statement (Greene p.97)

- Generally avoid OOD.
- Exceptions: ♭9 OK if ♭7 is present; ♭2 OK over a pedal; 3 & 11 bad, but 4 & 10 fine (same pattern for 11 & 17).

## Substitute / change-of-position terminology

- **C.O.P.** — Change of Position (chord inversion, but read across the bar line).
- **S.P.** — Substitute Position (used when no tone is held over from one chord to the next).
- **S.V.L.** — Substitute Voice-Leading.
- **V.L.** — Voice-Leading.

## Six types of Non-Chord Tones (NCTs)

- P.T. — Passing Tone.
- C.A. — Chromatic Alteration.
- R.T. — Returning Tone (neighbor).
- C.T. — Chord Tone (used as a passing/substitute chord tone in the bass).
- Sus. — Suspension.
- Ant. — Anticipation.

## Bar line as an "integrating force"

- Everything inside a single bar shares a single Root tone, scale, chord, and NCT set.
- VL and root-progression concerns only apply **across** the bar line.
- When you cross a bar, everything re-evaluates against the new Root.

## Passing Thirds and Fifths in the bass

- Inserted between two tonics.
- For Passing 3rds: Root must appear first.
- For Passing 5ths: no such ordering constraint.
- If a passing 3rd sounds metrically simultaneously with a treble structure, double the Root or 5th in the treble and omit the 3rd.
- Bass tones may be repeated one octave higher or lower (e.g. T 8-3 5).
- Watch parallel octaves between bass and any treble note.

## Six-Four chord

- Treated as a separate chord with the Root in the bass.
- Resolves 6→5, 4→3 (or the reverse); in minor either 5 or 6.
- 6 and 4 are treated as Substitute Tones here.
